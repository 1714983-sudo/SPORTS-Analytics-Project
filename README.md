# SPORTS-Analytics-Project
This Project is to help the coaches make better decisions and reduce player injuries
# Player Load and Injury Risk Analytics Tool
## Project Overview
This project proposes a Player Load and Injury Risk Analytics Tool for a professional soccer organization. The tool aggregates GPS tracking data, session workload metrics, and biometric indicators to generate a composite injury risk score for each player on a daily basis. Rather than relying solely on subjective coach assessments or reactive medical evaluations, this tool provides an evidence-based signal that helps the organization make proactive decisions about training load, rest, and squad selection.
## Decision-Making Problem
Professional soccer clubs face a persistent challenge in balancing player fitness, performance output, and injury prevention across a congested match schedule. Poor management of player load is one of the leading contributors to soft-tissue injuries, which not only harm player welfare but also compromise tactical options and impose significant financial costs through lost playing time. The central decision this tool supports is: which players require reduced training load, modified session participation, or complete rest on any given day?
This decision currently sits across the coaching staff, fitness coaches, and the medical department, often with incomplete or siloed information. A unified analytics tool would support more coordinated and evidence-grounded load management decisions.
## Proposed Analytics Approach
The tool would draw on three primary data streams. First, GPS and accelerometer data collected during training and matches would quantify physical output across dimensions such as total distance, high-intensity running distance, sprint count, and acceleration load. Second, session rating of perceived exertion (sRPE) data, collected daily from players via a brief digital survey, would provide a subjective workload measure. Third, heart rate variability (HRV) data collected through wearable devices would serve as a physiological indicator of recovery status.
These inputs would be combined into a rolling composite risk score using a weighted index, with thresholds calibrated against historical injury occurrence data from the club and comparable external datasets. No real-time modeling implementation is required at this stage; the approach is defined conceptually to inform prototyping and stakeholder engagement.
## Use by Decision Makers
The primary users of this tool would be the head coach, the performance and fitness coaching staff, and the medical department. Each morning, these stakeholders would receive a simple dashboard view showing each squad player's risk score for that day, color-coded across a low, moderate, and elevated risk scale. The head coach could use this information when designing the day's training session, modifying intensity or volume for flagged players. The fitness coach would use it to tailor individual conditioning work, and the medical staff would use elevated risk flags as a trigger for proactive check-ins or physiological assessments. The tool is intentionally designed to inform judgment rather than replace it, preserving coach autonomy while equipping decision makers with richer evidence.

## Project Prototype Enhancement
The original tool design relies on a composite risk score delivered once per morning. This prototype enhancement proposes the addition of a **contextual fatigue adjustment layer** that accounts for match schedule density when calculating each player's risk score.A secondary output of this enhancement would be a weekly fixture congestion 
report delivered to the head coach every Monday morning, summarizing which players are entering a high-risk window based on the upcoming schedule and their accumulated load from the previous seven days.
**What is being changed:** The current risk score model treats each day in isolation, weighting GPS load, sRPE, and HRV data equally regardless of whether a match is scheduled in 48 hours or eight days. The enhancement introduces a schedule-aware multiplier that dynamically increases the weight assigned to accumulated load metrics during congested fixture periods, defined as three or more matches within a ten-day window. During these periods, even moderate load readings would generate a higher risk flag, prompting earlier protective intervention.
**Why this change could improve decision-making:** Soft-tissue injury rates in professional soccer are disproportionately elevated during congested fixture periods, which suggests that a uniform risk threshold fails to capture the additional physiological stress of compressed recovery windows. By making the model sensitive to the match calendar, coaches and fitness staff would receive appropriately escalated warnings during the periods when squad management decisions are most consequential. This would make the tool more organizationally useful during the demanding parts of a season, which is precisely when the cost of getting load management wrong is highest.
